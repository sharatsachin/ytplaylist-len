There are two kinds of people who click on a 200-video YouTube course: the ones who finish it, and the vast majority who quietly abandon it somewhere around video 12. This post is a practical, tested system for being in the first group — used against my own habits and refined over dozens of courses.

## Why most people quit at video 12

Course playlist abandonment follows a very predictable pattern. Motivation is high on day one, moderate on day two, negligible by day five. This is not a discipline problem. It is a planning problem.

Three specific traps that catch almost everyone:

1. **You never calculated the actual total time.** You saw "62 videos" and mentally translated it as "a couple weekends of work". You did not multiply 62 by an average length of 18 minutes and get 18 hours — most of a work week.
2. **You planned around an ideal day, not an average day.** You told yourself "I will watch two hours after work" when your actual post-work average is 45 minutes.
3. **You did not have a finish date.** With no target, any given day feels equally acceptable to skip.

The fix for all three is the same: **precompute the numbers before you start**.

## Step 1: Get the real total duration

Paste the playlist URL into [YouTube Playlist Length](/). You will get, in about a second:

- Exact total duration
- Duration at 1.25x, 1.5x, 1.75x, and 2x
- Average video length (useful for splitting into sessions)
- Video count (so you know whether the tool's 500-video cap applies — this is a self-imposed limit on API quota use, not an API restriction)

Write down the duration at your chosen speed. Not the fantasy speed. The speed at which you can still take notes and understand new material — for most people, 1.25x-1.5x for new content.

## Step 2: Choose a real cadence

Pick a **minimum** daily watch time you will actually hit on your worst days, not your best. If your worst day is 20 minutes, your baseline is 20 minutes.

For a 30-hour course at 1.5x, that is 20 effective hours, or 60 days at 20 minutes per day, or 30 days at 40 minutes per day, or 15 days at 80 minutes per day. Pick the row that matches your worst-day reality.

## Step 3: Pick which days of the week you watch

Almost no one watches literally every day. Be honest:

- **Weekday-only learners** (5 of 7 days): multiply calendar days by 7/5 = 1.4.
- **Weekend-only learners** (2 of 7 days): multiply by 3.5. Weekend-only rarely works for anything over 10 hours.
- **All 7 days**: aspirational for most; be sure you actually do this before assuming it.

Enter these into the [Watch Schedule card on the home page](/) and it will tell you the specific date you finish. Look at that date. If it is more than 3 months out, the course is too long or your cadence is too slow. Consider one of these adjustments:

- Skip introduction / setup videos and start from the meat.
- Watch at 2x for review content, 1x for new content — variable speed by section.
- Watch every video, but only take notes on ones you can imagine actually using.

## Step 4: Track progress visibly

The single largest predictor of completion is whether you can see how far you have gotten. This is why the Watch Schedule calculator shows a progress ring — it exists specifically to fight the "did I even make a dent?" feeling that kills consistency.

Every time you enter your latest video-watched count, the ring updates and the finish date moves. Watch the finish date move earlier as you go faster than plan. Watch it move later as you fall behind. Either signal is more motivating than a static "you're on lesson 34" indicator.

## The videos you should skip

A dirty secret of YouTube courses is that many playlists include content you should skip on purpose:

- **Intros and channel promo.** Skip.
- **Recap videos.** Skip if you took notes on the original.
- **Setup and installation walkthroughs** longer than 10 minutes. Read the docs instead; you will save an hour.
- **Any video with a Q&A format** on material you already understood. Skip.

Use the **range** input in the tool to see the duration excluding these sections — it can shave 15-25% off the total.

## What to do when you fall behind

Everyone falls behind. The technique that works is: **do not try to make it up in one day**. Doing 3 hours on Sunday to compensate for skipping the week feels virtuous but is actually the worst signal — it teaches you that binging is possible, which primes the next week's skipping.

Instead, extend the finish date by exactly the number of days you missed. The finish date is not sacred, but the daily minimum is.
